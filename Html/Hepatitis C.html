Hepatitis C (HCV)

Overview

Introduction:

Hepatitis C is a bloodborne viral infection that primarily affects the liver. Most acute infections are asymptomatic, and 75‚Äì85% progress to chronic disease. Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) cure >95% within 8‚Äì12 weeks, but there is no vaccine. Routine screening is recommended for adults at least once in a lifetime and for high‚Äërisk groups.

Cause:

Caused by the hepatitis C virus (HCV), an enveloped, positive‚Äësense RNA virus. Transmission occurs primarily through blood exposure (e.g., needles, transfusions before screening, unsafe medical practices).

Genotypes of HCV

HCV has multiple genotypes (1‚Äì6) with regional distribution. Genotype helps guide therapy selection and duration.

  • Most common in North America and Europe.
  • High cure rates with pangenotypic DAAs.
  • Prevalent in Asia and parts of the Middle East.
  • Excellent response to sofosbuvir/velpatasvir.
  • Common in Africa (G4) and Southeast Asia (G6).
  • Treatable with pangenotypic regimens.

HCV is an enveloped, single‚Äëstranded positive‚Äësense RNA virus in the Flaviviridae family (genus Hepacivirus).

  • Genome: ~9.6 kb RNA; high mutation rate.
  • Surface proteins: E1/E2 glycoproteins for entry.

Transmission

Blood exposure

Shared needles/equipment; transfusions before screening.

Healthcare exposures

Unsafe injections/medical procedures.

Sexual transmission

Lower than HBV/HIV; higher with HIV coinfection.

Perinatal

Mother to child (~6% risk, higher if HIV+).

Diseases / Complications

Chronic HCV can lead to progressive liver disease. Early detection and curative therapy prevent complications.

Early / Moderate
  • Elevated liver enzymes
  • Fatty liver (steatosis)
  • Extrahepatic: cryoglobulinemia
Severe / Chronic
  • Cirrhosis
  • Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)
  • Liver failure / transplant
High-Risk Groups
  • People who inject drugs
  • Patients on hemodialysis
  • HIV coinfection
  • Transfusions before screening era
  • Infants of HCV‚Äëpositive mothers

Prevention

1
Harm reduction

Needle/syringe programs; avoid sharing equipment.

2
Blood screening

Rigorous testing of blood products and medical equipment sterilization.

3
Test & treat

Screen adults at least once; treat with DAAs to prevent transmission.

4
Safe practices

Use personal grooming tools; avoid sharing razors/toothbrushes.

Treatment

General Care
  • Evaluate fibrosis (e.g., FibroScan) and comorbidities.
  • Avoid alcohol and hepatotoxic drugs.
  • Vaccinate against HAV and HBV if non‚Äëimmune.
Direct‚ÄëActing Antivirals (DAAs)

Pangenotypic regimens (e.g., sofosbuvir/velpatasvir, glecaprevir/pibrentasvir) for 8‚Äì12 weeks cure >95% of patients.

Advanced Disease

Manage complications of cirrhosis and screen for HCC; consider transplant in liver failure.

Early diagnosis and treatment prevent cirrhosis and cancer.
